## Test Case 1: Contract Law - Breach of Real Estate Purchase Agreement
**Jurisdiction:** Texas  
**Practice Area:** Contract Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Does my client have grounds to rescind a residential real estate purchase agreement based on the seller's failure to disclose foundation problems?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Buyers (Sarah and Michael Chen) signed a purchase agreement on September 15, 2025 for a home in Austin, Texas for $575,000
- Contract included standard Texas TREC disclosure requirements
- Seller (Robert Martinez) checked "No" on the Seller's Disclosure Notice for foundation issues
- During the option period (October 1-10, 2025), buyer's inspector found evidence of significant foundation settling
- Structural engineer's report (October 8, 2025) estimated $45,000-$65,000 in foundation repairs needed
- Public records obtained October 12, 2025 show seller made insurance claim in 2023 for foundation damage
- Seller's contractor invoices from 2023 show cosmetic repairs to cracks but no structural foundation work
- Closing scheduled for October 30, 2025
- Option period has expired, but earnest money ($15,000) is still in escrow

**Desired Outcome:**
Rescind the purchase agreement based on fraudulent concealment, recover earnest money, and potentially seek damages for inspection costs and lost opportunity.

## Test Case 2: Criminal Law - DUI with Rising BAC Defense
**Jurisdiction:** California  
**Practice Area:** Criminal Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can we challenge the admissibility of breath test results using a "rising blood alcohol" defense when my client's BAC was below the legal limit at the time of driving?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Client (Jessica Torres, age 28) was driving home from dinner on November 20, 2025 at 9:45 PM
- She consumed two glasses of wine between 9:00-9:30 PM (last drink at 9:30 PM)
- Officer initiated traffic stop at 9:52 PM for allegedly crossing the fog line
- Field sobriety tests administered at 10:05 PM
- Preliminary breath test (PAS) at roadside at 10:15 PM showed 0.06% BAC
- Client arrested and transported to station
- Evidentiary breath test at station at 10:55 PM showed 0.09% BAC
- Second breath test at 11:05 PM showed 0.10% BAC
- Client has no prior DUI convictions
- Restaurant receipt shows wine purchase at 9:28 PM
- Body cam footage shows 23-minute gap between stop and first PAS test

**Desired Outcome:**
Suppress the breath test results or obtain expert testimony showing BAC was below 0.08% at the time of driving, seek reduction to reckless driving or dismissal.

## Test Case 3: Employment Law - FMLA Interference and Retaliation
**Jurisdiction:** Federal (employee in Illinois)  
**Practice Area:** Employment Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Did my client's employer violate the Family and Medical Leave Act by denying FMLA leave and subsequently terminating her employment?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Employee (Amanda Richardson, age 34) worked as a Senior Accountant at MidWest Financial Corp (150 employees) for 3 years
- Employee diagnosed with breast cancer on August 15, 2025
- Employee submitted FMLA leave request on August 20, 2025 for surgery and chemotherapy
- HR initially approved 12 weeks of leave starting September 1, 2025
- On August 28, 2025, new CFO sent email stating company "cannot accommodate extended absences during Q4 busy season"
- HR retracted FMLA approval on August 29, 2025, offered only 2 weeks
- Employee provided doctor's certification documenting medical necessity for 12 weeks
- Employee proceeded with surgery on September 3, 2025 and began leave
- Employee terminated on September 15, 2025 for "job abandonment"
- Employee's position posted on LinkedIn on September 16, 2025
- Former supervisor sent text message on September 14: "Sorry about this, CFO wants you gone because of the time off"
- Employee had consistently "Exceeds Expectations" performance reviews for 3 years

**Desired Outcome:**
Sue for FMLA interference and retaliation, seek reinstatement, back pay, front pay, liquidated damages, and attorney's fees.

## Test Case 4: Intellectual Property - Trade Secret Misappropriation
**Jurisdiction:** Delaware  
**Practice Area:** Intellectual Property

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can we obtain a preliminary injunction to prevent a former employee from using our proprietary client database and marketing algorithms at a competing startup?

**Relevant Facts:**
- TechBoost Inc. (Delaware C-Corp) developed proprietary SaaS marketing platform over 5 years
- Former VP of Engineering (David Kumar) resigned on October 15, 2025 after 4 years of employment
- Kumar signed non-compete agreement (November 2021) prohibiting competition for 18 months within the marketing automation industry
- Kumar also signed confidentiality agreement with specific provisions about trade secrets
- Kumar had access to source code, client database (5,000+ enterprise clients), and proprietary algorithms
- On November 1, 2025, Kumar announced new startup "MarketAI Pro" on LinkedIn
- Company's forensic IT analysis (November 5, 2025) shows Kumar downloaded 50GB of proprietary data to external drive on October 10, 2025
- Downloaded files include: complete source code repository, client contact database, pricing strategies, and unreleased product roadmaps
- MarketAI Pro's beta product (launched November 20, 2025) contains nearly identical UI and feature set
- Three of TechBoost's key clients have already switched to MarketAI Pro
- Former colleague reports Kumar showed him "the same system we built at TechBoost" in demo

**Desired Outcome:**
Obtain temporary restraining order and preliminary injunction to stop operations, prevent use of trade secrets, and seek damages for misappropriation under Delaware Uniform Trade Secrets Act and federal Defend Trade Secrets Act.

## Test Case 5: Family Law - Relocation Custody Dispute
**Jurisdiction:** Florida  
**Practice Area:** Family Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can my client (the mother) relocate with the minor children from Florida to Colorado over the father's objection when there is a 50/50 shared custody arrangement?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Parents (Jennifer Walsh and Thomas Walsh) divorced in March 2023 in Miami-Dade County
- Two children: Emma (age 8) and Noah (age 6)
- Current custody: 50/50 time-sharing with week-on/week-off schedule since divorce
- Both parents live within 5 miles of each other in Miami
- Mother has been offered a significant promotion (from $85,000 to $145,000 annual salary) at her company's Denver office
- Promotion would start February 1, 2026
- Mother filed Notice of Intent to Relocate on November 15, 2025
- Father filed objection on December 1, 2025
- Children currently attend excellent public school in Miami with strong performance records
- Mother's fiancé (engaged October 2025) lives in Denver and has a stable career there
- Father has been actively involved parent - coaches Emma's soccer team, attends all school events
- Mother's parents (children's grandparents) live in Miami and provide regular childcare
- Children have expressed to therapist they want to stay close to both parents but are excited about "mountain adventures"
- Father travels for work approximately 6 days per month for his job as pharmaceutical sales rep

**Desired Outcome:**
Obtain court approval for relocation to Colorado with revised custody arrangement (majority time with mother, holiday/summer visitation with father, virtual visitation schedule).

## Test Case 6: Real Estate Law - Adverse Possession Claim
**Jurisdiction:** Oregon  
**Practice Area:** Real Estate

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Has my client acquired title to a disputed strip of land through adverse possession under Oregon law?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Client (Robert Chen) purchased residential property at 245 Oak Street, Portland in 1996
- Property deed shows lot dimensions as 50 feet wide × 120 feet deep
- Client has maintained and used an additional 8-foot strip on the eastern boundary since 1996
- The 8-foot strip legally belongs to neighboring property at 247 Oak Street
- Client erected a wooden fence along what he believed was the property line in 1997, enclosing the 8-foot strip
- Client has maintained grass, planted trees, and installed an irrigation system on the disputed strip since 1996
- Client built a storage shed partially on the disputed strip in 2003 (with building permit that incorrectly showed property boundaries)
- Client paid property taxes on assessed value that included the disputed strip
- Neighbor at 247 Oak Street (owner since 2020) just had survey done (November 2025) revealing the encroachment
- New neighbor sent demand letter on December 1, 2025 requiring removal of fence, shed, and trees within 30 days
- Client's title insurance policy excludes boundary disputes based on survey
- Previous owner of 247 Oak Street (2010-2020) never objected to fence location
- Oregon requires 10 years of continuous adverse possession

**Desired Outcome:**
Establish title to the disputed 8-foot strip through adverse possession by bringing quiet title action, or alternatively negotiate purchase of the strip from neighbor at fair market value.

## Test Case 7: Tax Law - IRS Penalty Abatement
**Jurisdiction:** Federal  
**Practice Area:** Tax Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can my client obtain penalty abatement for failure to file and failure to pay penalties when the late filing was due to documented medical emergencies and reasonable cause?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Taxpayer (Maria Santos, sole proprietor of consulting business) failed to file 2023 federal income tax return by April 15, 2024 deadline
- Taxpayer suffered severe stroke on March 28, 2024, hospitalized until April 22, 2024
- Taxpayer was sole signer on business accounts and had not delegated tax filing responsibilities
- Medical records document cognitive impairment and recovery period through July 2024
- Taxpayer's CPA (who normally prepared returns) retired in February 2024 without notification
- Taxpayer filed 2023 return on September 15, 2024 (5 months late) after recovering
- Return showed tax liability of $42,000
- Taxpayer paid full tax liability on September 15, 2024
- IRS assessed penalties: failure to file ($9,450), failure to pay ($3,780), total penalties $13,230
- Taxpayer has clean compliance history: filed all returns on time for previous 15 years, no prior penalties
- Taxpayer submitted formal penalty abatement request on October 1, 2024 with medical documentation
- IRS denied abatement request on December 10, 2024 stating "insufficient documentation of reasonable cause"
- Taxpayer has additional medical records and physician affidavit not included in original request

**Desired Outcome:**
File administrative appeal to obtain full abatement of failure-to-file and failure-to-pay penalties based on reasonable cause (serious illness), or prepare for Tax Court petition if appeal denied.

## Test Case 8: Immigration Law - Deportation Defense
**Jurisdiction:** Federal (removal proceedings in New York)  
**Practice Area:** Immigration

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can my client avoid deportation through cancellation of removal for non-permanent residents based on exceptional and extremely unusual hardship to his U.S. citizen children?

**Relevant Facts:**
- Client (Miguel Rodriguez, age 38) is citizen of Mexico, entered U.S. without inspection in 2007 (18 years ago)
- Client has three U.S. citizen children: Sofia (age 14), Carlos (age 11), and Ana (age 8)
- Children's mother (U.S. citizen) died in car accident in 2022
- Client is sole caregiver and financial provider for children
- Client has worked continuously as construction foreman (same company for 12 years)
- Sofia has Type 1 diabetes requiring careful management and regular medical care
- Carlos has autism spectrum disorder, receives specialized education services and therapy
- Ana is healthy but very young and emotionally dependent on father
- Client has no criminal record, no prior immigration violations
- Client detained by ICE during traffic stop on November 1, 2025
- Notice to Appear issued November 5, 2025 for removal proceedings
- Client has been in U.S. continuously for 11 years (exceeds 10-year requirement)
- Children currently staying with client's sister, but she works full-time and cannot be permanent guardian
- Removal to Mexico would mean children either: (a) go to Mexico (disrupting medical care, education, support systems), or (b) remain in U.S. without parent
- Children's therapist and school officials prepared to testify about father's critical role and impact of separation

**Desired Outcome:**
Obtain cancellation of removal under INA §240A(b) demonstrating exceptional and extremely unusual hardship to U.S. citizen children if father is deported.

## Test Case 9: Environmental Law - Clean Water Act Violation
**Jurisdiction:** Federal (facility in Louisiana)  
**Practice Area:** Environmental Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Is our manufacturing company liable under the Clean Water Act for stormwater discharge that exceeded permit limits, and what defenses are available given the discharge was caused by Hurricane Laura?

**Relevant Facts:**
- GulfCoast Manufacturing operates chemical plant in Lake Charles, Louisiana under NPDES permit
- Facility has stormwater discharge permit with specific pollutant limits
- Hurricane Laura made landfall on August 27, 2025 as Category 4 hurricane
- Hurricane caused catastrophic damage to facility's stormwater containment system
- Secondary containment berms breached during storm surge
- Stormwater discharge during and after hurricane (August 27-29, 2025) contained elevated levels of petroleum products and suspended solids
- Company's sampling on August 30, 2025 showed exceedances: Total Suspended Solids (450 mg/L vs 100 mg/L limit), Oil & Grease (35 mg/L vs 15 mg/L limit)
- Company immediately notified EPA Region 6 of discharge exceedances on August 30, 2025
- EPA inspection on September 15, 2025 documented conditions
- EPA issued Notice of Violation on October 20, 2025
- Company repaired containment system by September 30, 2025 (cost: $2.3 million)
- Company's stormwater pollution prevention plan was current and properly implemented pre-hurricane
- Company had emergency response procedures in place
- No other violations in facility's 15-year operating history
- EPA is proposing $175,000 in civil penalties
- Local environmental groups are threatening citizen suit for additional damages

**Desired Outcome:**
Negotiate settlement with EPA that acknowledges force majeure defense, reduces penalties based on good faith efforts and compliance history, and precludes citizen suits through federal enforcement action.

## Test Case 10: Corporate Law - Shareholder Derivative Action
**Jurisdiction:** Delaware  
**Practice Area:** Corporate Law

**Legal Issue/Question:**
Can minority shareholders bring a derivative action against the board of directors for breach of fiduciary duty related to self-dealing transactions, or does the demand requirement prevent the suit?

**Relevant Facts:**
- TechVenture Inc. is Delaware C-Corporation, founded 2019
- Company has 5 board members: CEO John Mitchell (40% ownership), CFO Sarah Lee (10% ownership), and 3 independent directors
- Plaintiff shareholders (collectively 15% ownership) discovered CEO sold commercial real estate property to company in March 2025
- Property purchased by company for $4.2 million, allegedly "fair market value"
- Independent appraisal obtained by plaintiffs (November 2025) values property at $2.8 million
- CEO personally purchased the same property in 2023 for $2.1 million
- Board approved transaction on March 10, 2025 with CEO recused from vote (4-0 approval)
- Board meeting minutes show "independent appraisal" supported $4.2M value, but that appraisal was commissioned by CEO's personal attorney
- CFO received $50,000 "consulting fee" from CEO's personal LLC two weeks before the board vote
- Two of three "independent" directors have significant business relationships with CEO's other ventures
- Company's financial condition has deteriorated: revenue down 30% in 2025, recent layoffs of 40 employees
- Plaintiff shareholders sent demand letter to board on November 1, 2025 requesting rescission of property purchase
- Board's special litigation committee (formed November 15, 2025) rejected demand on December 5, 2025 stating transaction was "entirely fair"
- Special litigation committee consisted of 2 directors with business ties to CEO

**Desired Outcome:**
File derivative action alleging breach of fiduciary duty, seeking rescission of property transaction or damages of $1.4 million plus prejudgment interest, and argue demand futility to overcome demand requirement.
